history

The history of human civilization began with the transition from nomadic hunting and gathering societies to settled agricultural communities, around 10,000 BCE. This shift, known as the Neolithic Revolution, led to the rise of the first cities in regions such as Mesopotamia, Egypt, the Indus Valley, and China. With the ability to produce surplus food, societies became more complex, allowing for the development of social classes, trade, art, and written language. Mesopotamia, often referred to as the "Cradle of Civilization," saw the emergence of the first known cities and one of the earliest systems of writing: cuneiform. This era laid the groundwork for modern society, as people organized themselves into structured communities, governed by rulers and regulated by laws.

Another significant period in history was the Age of Exploration, which began in the 15th century. Driven by the desire for new trade routes, wealth, and knowledge, European explorers such as Christopher Columbus, Vasco da Gama, and Ferdinand Magellan ventured into uncharted territories. This period had profound impacts on global history, as it initiated widespread cultural exchanges, the establishment of European colonies, and the transatlantic slave trade. The Age of Exploration connected distant continents, but it also brought about centuries of conflict, colonization, and exploitation that shaped the political and social landscapes of the modern world.
